The first thing people ask about BrainScribe is some version of: *isn't this just AI doing the homework?*

It's the right question. Most AI writing tools will happily hand a student five finished paragraphs. That's not help — that's a kid turning in work they didn't do, and learning nothing. We built BrainScribe specifically so that can't happen.

## It never writes a word for them

This is the line we won't cross, and it's built into how the product works — not a setting you can flip off.

BrainScribe doesn't generate sentences for the student to copy. It asks questions. The student answers — by voice or by typing — and the coach reflects their own words back, tidies them into a clean paragraph, and asks the student to approve it. Every sentence in the final essay traces back to something the student actually said.

Take away the student's answers and there's nothing left. The coach has no essay of its own to give.

## You can read exactly how it happened

Every session produces a transcript — the questions the coach asked and the answers the student gave, start to finish. Parents and teachers linked to a student can read the whole thing.

So you're never taking anyone's word for it. If you want to know whether the work is really theirs, you can see the conversation that produced it.

## Teachers weren't just okay with it — they were relieved

When this started, it was one parent and one phone, helping a stuck kid finish an essay at 11pm (that's [the whole origin story](/about), if you haven't read it). The next morning, we told the teacher exactly what we'd done and offered the transcript.

She wasn't upset. She was relieved — because for the first time she could see a kid who usually freezes actually working through his ideas, in his own words, and finishing.

That's the bar. Not "can a student produce an essay" — anything can do that now. The bar is: *did the student do the thinking, and can you tell?* With BrainScribe, the answer is yes, and you can.
