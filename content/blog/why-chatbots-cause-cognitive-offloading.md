In [Part 1](/blog/not-cognitive-offloading) we split writing into six steps and drew the line: cognitive offloading is handing over the *thinking* — steps 1 through 4 — not the mechanics. General chatbots land squarely on the wrong side of that line. Here's why, and why the usual fixes don't work.

## A one-line prompt buys a whole essay

Ask a chatbot to "write a five-paragraph essay on the causes of the Civil War," and it does all four thinking steps — the ideas, what matters, the structure, the wording — in a few seconds. The student's entire contribution is the prompt. Everything writing is supposed to build, the machine did instead. That's cognitive offloading in its purest form: not a little help at the edges, but the whole cognitive job handed off.

## "Just tell kids not to" doesn't hold

It's free, it's on every phone, and it's frictionless — and the moment an assignment feels too hard or a deadline is too close, the easy path is right there. That's not a character flaw in your kid; it's what happens when the shortcut is one tap away and the hard way is a blank page. Willpower is a thin defense against a tool that convenient.

## Detectors make it worse, not better

So schools reach for AI detectors. The trouble is they don't work reliably. OpenAI quietly shut down its own AI-text detector in 2023, citing low accuracy.

And the false alarms aren't random. A 2023 Stanford study found these detectors flagged more than half of essays written by non-native English speakers as AI-generated. So the tool meant to catch cheating ends up accusing some of the most honest, hardest-working kids — while still missing the ones who lightly reworded a chatbot's draft. A defense that punishes the wrong students isn't a defense.

## "Guided" AI is better — but the guardrail is a suggestion

Newer "study" modes that promise to tutor instead of answer are a real step up, and worth taking seriously. But the thing stopping them from writing the essay is a *policy* — an instruction telling the model to hold back. A kid who's stuck can open a second tab and paste the assignment into the plain version. A guardrail that lives in a prompt is one a motivated 14-year-old can walk around.

## What actually gets lost

Here's the part that matters more than any grade: writing is one of the main ways kids learn to *think* — to take a fuzzy opinion and sharpen it into a claim with reasons behind it. Every essay is reps for that skill. Offload the thinking to a chatbot and you don't just lose the assignment — you lose the reps. The kid hands in an essay and learns nothing about how to make one.

Which raises the real question, and it's the one [Part 3](/blog/how-coaching-avoids-cognitive-offloading) is about: can an AI actually help a struggling writer *without* taking over the thinking?
