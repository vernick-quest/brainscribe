title: "Why we call it a coach, not a tutor"
date: 2026-07-11
summary: "Tutor" and "coach" sound interchangeable, but they name two different jobs — and the difference is the whole reason BrainScribe works the way it does.
tag: How it works
---

People call BrainScribe an "AI tutor" all the time. We get why — it's the nearest familiar word. But we don't use it, and the reason isn't branding. A tutor and a coach do two different jobs, and which one you're actually after changes everything about what your kid walks away with.

**A tutor closes a gap.** They explain the thing you don't understand, work the example, and hand you the answer when you're stuck. That's exactly what you want when the problem is *you don't know something* — a tricky bit of algebra, a grammar rule that never clicked.

**A coach builds a capability.** A coach doesn't run onto the field and take the shot for you. They ask, they watch, they adjust, and they make you do the reps. The point isn't winning today's game — it's that you can play the next one without them.

For writing — especially for a kid with ADHD — the gap usually isn't knowledge. Your kid isn't missing facts about their book. They know what they think; they'll tell you all about it at dinner. What breaks is getting it *out* and *down*: starting, holding the thread, not abandoning it halfway. A "tutor" that hands over sentences fixes tonight's assignment and leaves the actual skill exactly where it was. And with AI, handing over sentences is one tap away — which is the whole reason so many parents and teachers are uneasy about it.

A coach can't hand anything over. Ours asks questions until your kid says the thing they mean, then helps them get their *own* words onto the page — tidied up, never written for them. Every sentence traces back to something they said out loud. That's slower than being handed a paragraph. It's also the only version where the skill, and the work, stay theirs.

There's a quieter reason the word matters, too. "Tutor" carries a faint whiff of *remedial* — something's wrong, and we're here to fix it. But plenty of the kids who do best here aren't behind at all. They're sharp, fast talkers who just stall at the keyboard. "Coach" doesn't come with that baggage. You don't get a coach because you're broken. You get one because you want to get better, and having someone in your corner makes that easier.

So: not a tutor. A coach. It's a small word. But it's the difference between doing your kid's homework and helping them become someone who can do it themselves.
