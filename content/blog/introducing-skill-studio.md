Every assignment in BrainScribe is a real one. There's a due date, a grade, a topic the student didn't choose. That's the point — coaching where the stakes are real is how a kid learns to finish the work in front of them.

But you don't get better at basketball by only ever playing games. You get better in practice — running the same move until it's yours, where a missed shot costs nothing. Writing works the same way. So we built the gym.

## What Skill Studio is

Skill Studio is a low-stakes place to sharpen one writing skill at a time. No grade, no deadline, no essay riding on it — just short reps on a single thing, with the same coaches your student already knows.

There are 24 skills, grouped into three tiers:

- **Foundations** — the moves every piece of writing needs: a hook that pulls a reader in, specific detail instead of vague filler, a closing line that lands.
- **Structure** — how a piece holds together: topic sentences, evidence, analysis, a thesis, transitions.
- **Craft** — the finishing work: tone, revision, timed writing, finding a voice that's unmistakably theirs.

A student works one skill, then the next one unlocks. You build the wall a brick at a time — you can't skip to analysis before you've practiced getting a claim and its evidence down. As skills stack up, so does a ladder of milestones: **Scribe**, then **Wordsmith**, then **Stylist**, then **Virtuoso**. Every rep a student finishes goes into a portfolio they can look back on — proof, in their own words, of how far they've come.

## Where the skills come from

These 24 skills aren't something we invented. When we built Skill Studio, we studied how writing is actually taught — the approaches with the strongest track record in real classrooms, especially for kids who find writing hard. Three ideas shaped what you see:

- In **Foundations**, every skill is an explicit, named move a student can remember and reach for — and each session ends with one small reflection: *what's the one thing you'll try to remember this week?* Practice sticks when a kid can name what they practiced.
- In **Structure**, writing is treated as entering a conversation. Before a student argues a point, the coach asks the two questions good writers always ask: *what do most people think about this — and what do you think?*
- In **Craft**, students pick up a shared vocabulary for talking about good writing — ideas, organization, voice, word choice — so they can look at their own draft and see what to sharpen next.

We built our own version of all of it: our own moves, our own words, our own memory anchors, sequenced the way a writer really grows. Teachers who know the research will recognize the DNA. A student just sees skills they can practice, one rep at a time.

## Same promise as the rest of BrainScribe

The gym coaches exactly the way the assignments do. The coach asks; the student answers, by voice or by typing; and their own words get tidied into something clean they approve. Nothing gets written for them here either. A student walks out of Skill Studio with a paragraph they made, not one they were handed.

That's the whole idea. The gym isn't a shortcut around the writing — it's more writing, on purpose, in the one setting where a missed rep costs nothing and confidence is allowed to build slowly.

## Who it's for

For the kid who freezes at the start of every essay, Skill Studio is a place to practice starting where it doesn't count. For the perfectionist, it's permission to write a rough rep and move on. For the strong writer who's bored, it's the harder question — a Craft skill they haven't beaten yet.

Assignments show a student they can finish tonight's work. The gym shows them they're becoming a writer. Both are theirs.
