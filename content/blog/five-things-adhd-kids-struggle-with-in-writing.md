If you've watched your kid sit in front of a blank document for an hour, you already know the script. The assignment isn't hard. They have things to say. And yet nothing comes out — and somewhere around minute forty, it starts to look like they're just not trying.

They're trying. Writing pulls on exactly the parts of the brain that ADHD makes harder to reach. Here are five of them.

## 1. Getting started

A blank page is a hundred decisions at once — what to say first, how to begin, whether the idea is even good. For a kid with ADHD, that pile of decisions can be paralyzing before a single word is written. It's not procrastination. It's the cost of starting being higher than it looks from the outside.

## 2. Holding the idea long enough to write it down

Your kid might have a great sentence in their head. By the time they've worked out the spelling and the punctuation and where the comma goes, the sentence is gone. Working memory is doing two jobs at once — *hold the idea* and *transcribe it* — and one of them usually loses.

## 3. Getting unstuck once they stall

Everyone stalls mid-essay. Most kids nudge themselves forward. ADHD makes that nudge harder to find — so a stall becomes a full stop, and a full stop at 9pm becomes a meltdown at 10:30.

## 4. Order and structure

The ideas are there, but they arrive out of order — paragraph three first, the intro last, a great detail with nowhere to put it. Turning a swirl of thoughts into a straight line is its own skill, and it's a hard one.

## 5. Editing without erasing everything

When a kid can't tell *this sentence is fine* from *this whole thing is bad*, editing becomes deleting. They write a sentence, decide it's wrong, delete it, and start over — sometimes for hours.

## What actually helps

Notice the through-line: none of these is a lack of ideas. They're all about getting the ideas *out* — past the blank page, past working memory, past the stall.

That's the whole reason BrainScribe works the way it does. Instead of a blank page, your kid gets a coach asking one question at a time. They answer out loud — talking is far easier than transcribing — and what they say gets tidied into a paragraph they approve. The ideas were always theirs. The coach just holds the door open so they can get them out.
